BHF Berlin

Urban Vision and Concept:
As a utopian model for urban design, the castle appeared to be a suitable starting point. Its isolation and inner density inspired a reinterpretation of urban form, resisting the freneticism of contemporary cities while retaining vibrancy. Drawing inspiration from the historic city of Split, Croatia—where a medieval city inhabits the ruins of Diocletian’s Palace—the proposal explores the interplay between rigidity and spontaneity, between old and new.

This idea is further rooted in Berlin’s own urban heritage. The perimeter block typology of the late 19th century—with its shielded interior courtyards—serves as a base for the design. The dense fabric is laid over the site and then opened selectively to form new sub-blocks, generating a porous, evolving urban system. In contrast to large-scale developments, the proposal advocates for small plot divisions to foster diversity and social resilience.

The city becomes a mixed-use district, with a strong emphasis on student housing, local commerce, public courtyards, and community infrastructure. Amidst noise constraints near the rail line, office space is prioritised, while David Bowie Square is programmed as a vibrant public hub anchored by a school. Public and semi-private spaces are structured through streets, alleys, and courtyards—punctuated with vegetation extending from courtyards onto the street. These green fingers visually and functionally connect Hardenbergplatz, the new neighbourhood, and the rail viaduct. The result is a richly layered, small-scale urbanism that channels Berlin’s history into a vision for a liveable post-pandemic city.
